## Race Information

### Society

#### Echoes of the Divine

##### Integration into Larger Societies

Aasimar were exceedingly rare across Toril and, like other planetouched, had no true cities or communities of their own. Many could live their entire lives without ever encountering another of their kind, and thus were accustomed to residing among other races. Only a handful of aasimar had siblings who shared their celestial heritage, both because such unions between celestials or deities and mortals were uncommon and because ancient aasimar bloodlines had long since faded into dormancy. Consequently, encounters between aasimar were extremely rare, but when they did occur, a profound sense of kinship and unspoken understanding often arose. Across the spectrum, aasimar instinctively tended to support one another, creating bonds that transcended personal differences.

##### Lone Guardians

Growing up apart from others of their kind, many aasimar developed a deep sense of independence and self-reliance. This solitude often shaped them into vigilant and thoughtful guardians, their lives marked by both quiet resilience and an acute awareness of the responsibilities their celestial heritage imposed.

##### Bonds Beyond Blood

Despite their human ancestry, aasimar did not usually feel a particularly strong attachment to their mortal kin. Instead, they often found deeper connections with other half-breeds or those who shared a measure of otherworldly heritage. Many enjoyed the company of races as varied as half-elves or half-orcs, though very few formed lasting bonds with tieflings, toward whom they instinctively felt wariness. Genasi, too, seemed alien to most aasimar, appearing strange even by the celestial-descended race's own standards. Among other common races, aasimar generally held little strong opinion, preferring measured observation to rash judgment.

##### Burden of Destiny

Most aasimar grew up cautious in the presence of others, and like tieflings, were sometimes misunderstood; though rarely to the hateful extremes often directed at fiendish bloodlines. Even those nurtured by patient and understanding parents could not entirely escape their innate strangeness, nor the curiosity, or, at times, fear, that their celestial nature inspired. Prejudice was not uncommon, and it struck deeply at the hearts of many aasimar, who were naturally empathetic toward others. To avoid unnecessary danger, they often maintained a low profile in public, wary of attracting the attention of evil cultists, fiends, or other malevolent forces drawn to their divine essence. Yet when the cause was just, an aasimar would not hesitate to act against evil, always careful to shield the innocent from harm.

### Regions

##### Celestial-Touched Children of Toril

Aasimar were scattered and rare throughout Faerûn, often living unnoticed among mortal populations rather than forming settlements of their own. Their divine heritage set them apart, but their small numbers meant that most Aasimar grew up integrated into human, elven, or other mortal communities. In cities, they could be found quietly working as scholars, clerics, healers, or adventurers, while in rural areas, they often lived simple lives under the guise of ordinary mortals. Aasimar were drawn to places where their unique gifts could be used to protect others, offer guidance, or pursue divine missions, often gravitating toward centers of learning, temples, and communities with strong moral frameworks.

##### Celestial Guardians of Unther and Mulhorand

Some Aasimar traced their ancestry to the lands of Unther and Mulhorand, regions steeped in ancient celestial traditions and divine worship. Their divine nature inspired respect and devotion, and many felt a deep sense of purpose in safeguarding the people and upholding the sacred laws of the region.

##### Hidden Lights of the Sword Coast

Along the Sword Coast, Aasimar could be found scattered in both bustling cities and quiet frontier towns. In places like Waterdeep, Baldur's Gate, and Neverwinter, their abilities and otherworldly appearances were less likely to draw unwanted attention, allowing them to serve as healers, sages, or champions of justice. Some aasimar preferred more remote locales, using their powers to aid isolated communities or confront hidden evils. Though few in number, their presence left a lasting impression, and tales of mysterious protectors often spread among local populations, blending into the fabric of the region's legends and folklore.

### Appearance

**Human-Based: **Aasimar generally resembled humans in size and basic features, sharing similar heights and builds. What set them apart were subtle but striking differences: unusual skin tones, distinctive eye colors, and occasionally a faint, otherworldly glow that hinted at their celestial heritage.

**Celestial Touched:** Many aasimar bore unmistakable marks of their divine ancestry. Their hair or eyes might shimmer in gold or silver hues, and body markings, resembling tattoos or natural patterns, could glimmer faintly. Some displayed more unusual traits, such as emerald-toned skin or feathers woven into their hair, while others possessed pupil-less eyes or other rare physical features that reflected their celestial lineage. These signs of otherworldly influence often made aasimar appear simultaneously familiar and extraordinary to mortal eyes.

#### Varied Appearances

##### Protector Aasimar

Protector Aasimar were typically tall and well-built, their physiques closely resembling those of humans. What distinguished them, however, were their radiant eyes and subtly glowing skin. Hair and eye colors often shimmered in gold or silver tones, and they exuded a calming, protective aura that inspired trust and confidence in those around them.

##### Scourge Aasimar

Scourge Aasimar bore a similar physique to Protectors, but their appearance often carried more intense or striking features. Hair and eyes could lean toward bluish tones rather than gold or silver. Many Scourge Aasimar preferred to wear masks or other coverings to conceal their otherworldly traits, revealing their true nature only in the heat of battle.

##### Fallen Aasimar

In contrast to their Protector or Scourge counterparts, Fallen Aasimar often displayed shadowy, ashen, or unusually pale skin, reflecting their estrangement from celestial light. Their eyes could be entirely black, deep red, or possess a smoldering intensity, sometimes resembling burning coals or voids of darkness. Fallen Aasimar often radiated an unsettling or ominous aura, making their presence disquieting to those nearby, in stark contrast to the comforting or inspiring effect typical of other aasimar.

### Faith

Because of their ties to benevolent gods and celestial beings, many aasimar are naturally drawn to a religious path, and most aasimar spellcasters channel divine rather than arcane magic. While many are devoted to gods of goodness, protection, or justice, individual aasimar may follow a wide range of deities depending on their upbringing, experiences, and personal convictions.

#### Mulhorandi Pantheon

Many aasimar venerate the Mulhorandi pantheon, with a notable portion of the race tracing their lineage to the goodly gods of Mulhorand. These aasimar often feel a particular connection to the animals or natural domains under the protection of their divine ancestors. The pantheon, centered in Heliopolis, includes:

- Anhur, god of war and weather; often associated with animals used for combat and hunting.

- Bast, goddess of hedonism, festhalls, love, and cats. She is called Sharess and is worshiped by that name outside of Mulhorand.

- Bes, god of chance and luck.

- Geb, god of the earth, minerals, and mining.

- Hathor, goddess of fertility and motherhood.

- Horus-Re, the leader of the Mulhorandi pantheon, he is the god of the sun, kings, rulership, and life.

- Isis, goddess of good magic and agriculture.

- Nephthys, goddess of trade and wealth.

- Osiris, god of death and the dead.

- Re, former leader of the Mulhorandi pantheon, he was slain in a battle and his power was subsumed by Horus, who then became Horus-Re.

- Sebek, god of wetland and river hazards, danger.

- Set, god of evil, drought, destruction, and the desert.

- Thoth, god of knowledge, secrets, and academic debate.

#### The Triad

The Triad is an alliance of three good-aligned deities: Tyr, the Maimed God, god of justice; Ilmater, the Broken God, god of suffering and compassion; and Torm, the True, god of duty. Aasimar may follow the Triad collectively or honor a single member. Those devoted to Tyr pursue justice and righteousness, often serving as judges, champions, or enforcers. Followers of Ilmater dedicate themselves to alleviating suffering and offering compassion to the afflicted. Aasimar who revere Torm seek to embody loyalty and courage, standing against evil and protecting the helpless.

#### Evil Gods for Fallen

Some aasimar, straying from their celestial path, turn toward evil due to feelings of alienation or the belief that their divine heritage has dictated their destiny. These Fallen Aasimar often lose their celestial patron, yet retain formidable power, their radiant energy twisting into necrotic or destructive force. Among the gods they might follow are:

**Shar: **The goddess of darkness and loss, Shar is a perfect fit for Fallen Aasimar who feel alienated and ostracized. They might find solace in the cold embrace of Shar, seeking to be a part of the mysteries of the night.

**Bane:** The god of tyranny, fear, and hatred, Bane might appeal to Fallen Aasimar who seek to impose their will upon others and use their fallen angelic powers for domination.
